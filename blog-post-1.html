The People Closest to You Can Be the Loudest Critics — And That's Worth Talking About

Sakshi Jhaveri 17th Apr 2026

A few months ago, I started a new practice. Not because I wanted to be good at it. Not because I had a goal to perform or compete. I started because I was uncomfortable in my own body, and I needed something that would help me feel at home in it again.

It was uncomfortable at first. I had never done anything like this before, and there were so many people around me who were so much better. But what kept me going was that those very people, my peers and trainers, made me feel safe enough to return, because it felt like this space was serving my very purpose of starting this journey. Every session ended with a big smile and a quiet sense of comfort I hadn't felt in a long time. It felt like I belonged there.

And it worked. Slowly, quietly, it worked.

What I didn't anticipate was the noise that would come from the people closest to me.

I remember it starting very early, when I was excited to share my early videos with them. The comments came quickly. Comments comparing me to others who had been doing this for years. Unsolicited advice. The people who I expected to simply witness me, showing up, trying, moving, became the loudest voices telling me I wasn't doing it right.

It felt like criticism. It felt, at times, like the people closest to me were making fun of me.

But in the same breath, I had people around who showed up differently. The ones who watched me grow, who never once compared me to anyone else in that frame. The ones who celebrated the fact that I was simply there, moving, trying. Their support didn't come with conditions or corrections; it came with genuine warmth and a quiet belief in me. That kind of encouragement is everything. This is what keeps me going on the days the noise gets louder. I am grateful for them more than they know.

Here's what I want you to know: I've done enough work on myself to not be derailed by the unsolicited advice, and it's still a work in progress. But I also know that not everyone is in that place yet. And that's exactly why I'm writing this.

In my work with athletes, exercisers, and people navigating rehabilitation, I see this pattern constantly.

Parents comparing their children to other athletes. Coaches pushed to increase training intensity because someone else's child is doing more. People returning to exercise after a long break, holding themselves up against others who never left, a habit, years of conditioning have quietly built in them. And those on a rehabilitation journey, measuring their recovery against someone else's timeline entirely.

What happens next is predictable and heartbreaking. The joy starts to leave. The 'why' behind the movement gets buried under the weight of comparison. And eventually, many of them stop altogether, not because they failed, but because the environment stopped feeling safe.

Judgment from the people you love doesn't motivate. It quietly dismantles. It lands as rejection.

There's a difference between feedback that helps someone grow and comparison that quietly tells them they're not good enough. One is rooted in belief. The other is rooted in a standard they never asked to be measured against.

If someone in your life has started something new, a movement practice, a sport, a rehab journey, or literally anything else, they didn't begin it to impress you. They began it for something much more personal. To have fun. To heal. To feel capable. To reclaim something. When you respond by pointing elsewhere, you're not pushing them forward. You're pulling them away from the very thing that was helping them.

To the people who love us and don't realise what they're doing:

This isn't an accusation. It's an invitation.

When someone shares their movement with you, a video, a story, a small win, they are not asking to be evaluated. They are asking to be seen. There is a profound difference between those two things. Seeing someone means acknowledging what it took for them to show up. Evaluating someone means measuring them against a standard that has nothing to do with their journey.

You don't need to be an expert to be supportive. You just need to resist the urge to compare and choose to witness instead.

And to the person who is still showing up despite the noise:

Keep going.

The fact that you're still showing up, still trying, choosing yourself in the face of discouragement, that is not a small thing. That is the whole thing.

The goal was never to look a certain way or perform at a certain level. The goal was to have fun, to feel better in your body, to build a relationship with movement that actually serves you. Someone else's words are a reflection of their own thoughts and limitations, not a measure of your worth or your progress.

Go back to your 'Why'. Why did you start? Hold on to that. Your competitor is you, not the person next to you. Everyone comes with a different story, and so do you.

And as you keep showing up, pay attention to who is in your corner. Your people are the ones who celebrate your journey, not just your results. They don't need to understand exactly what you're doing or why; they just need to believe in you while you figure it out. Seek those people out. Hold onto them. Let their belief in you be the thing you return to when the outside noise gets too loud. Gratitude for the right people in your corner is one of the most underrated tools you have.

Don't quit before you find out what you're capable of.
